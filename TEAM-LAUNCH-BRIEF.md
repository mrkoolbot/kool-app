## LEO 🦁 — Social Media Launch Strategy

### When to Launch: Social BEFORE App Store. Always.

**The sequence matters more than the date.**

Launching cold on the App Store is table stakes — you'll get buried by the algorithm with zero reviews and zero social proof. The right sequence is:

1. **Now → Day 60:** Social buildup. Content. Community. Followers.
2. **Day 60 checkpoint:** ≥2,000 Instagram followers + ≥500 TikTok followers.
3. **Day 61:** Soft launch App Store (invite-only beta via TestFlight).
4. **Day 90:** Full public App Store launch with 50+ reviews already in.

The App Store rewards apps that launch with momentum — downloads, ratings, retention signals in the first 72 hours. You can't manufacture that retroactively. You build it in the weeks before the button goes live.

## MEL ✍️ — Content & Positioning

### Is the Current App Store Listing Strong Enough?

**Honest assessment: 85%. Not quite there.**

The copy is clean, on-brand, and readable. The lowercase voice is right. The founder credibility angle (8+ years, built by a real planner) is the strongest thing in it. But it has three weaknesses:

**1. The opening line undersells.**
> *"your event doesn't have a second chance at a first impression."*

This is about *events*, not about *the planner's pain*. The person downloading this app is stressed. They're juggling 6 tools. They just got burned by a vendor. Open with THEM, not with the event.

**Recommended replacement:**
> *"you're running 6 apps just to plan one event. there's a better way."*

Or more emotionally:
> *"the spreadsheet at 11pm. the vendor who ghosted. the guest list that broke into three DM threads. you deserve better tools."*

**2. The feature list reads like a spec sheet.**
"Smart Planning Timelines. Expert Checklists. Guest Management." — these are features, not benefits. The competitor analysis shows planners' pain is emotional as much as functional. Reframe each feature around the relief it provides.

| Current | Better |
|---|---|
| Smart planning timelines | "KOOL builds your timeline the moment you create an event — because the clock starts immediately." |
| Expert checklists | "Checklists built from real events — not some PM's guess about what 'done' looks like." |
| Budget tracker | "No more end-of-event sticker shock. See exactly where every dollar went, in real time." |

**3. The free tier explanation is buried.**
"Free forever for intimate events" is hidden mid-description. This is a *conversion driver* — move it earlier, make it scannable.

---

### What's Missing from Onboarding Copy

The listing sells the download. The onboarding copy has to sell the *habit*. Currently, there's no documented onboarding flow. These screens need to be written before launch:

**Screen 1 — The Welcome:**
> *"welcome to kool. you plan the events. we hold the details."*
> *"let's build your first event together."*

**Screen 2 — Event Type (first decision):**
> *"what are you planning?"* → [Wedding] [Corporate Event] [Birthday] [Party] [Other]
> Sub-copy: *"your checklist, timeline, and budget template will be built for this exact event type."*

**Screen 3 — Date input:**
> *"when's the big day?"*
> Sub-copy: *"kool will count down from right now and tell you exactly what to do next."*

**Screen 4 — The Hook (after first event created):**
> *"your event is live. here's what needs to happen this week."*
> This is the moment KOOL proves its value. The user sees a real, contextualized checklist — not a blank template. This moment must be *magic*.

**Screen 5 — Free vs. Premium gate (when they hit the 25-guest limit):**
> *"your event is growing — nice problem to have."*
> *"upgrade to premium: unlimited guests, unlimited events, and tools built for professionals."*
> This is NOT a paywall. It's a celebration. Tone matters.

### How to Position KOOL Differently from Eventbrite/Partiful

These are not the same product. But users don't know that yet. Here's the positioning map:

| Platform | What they do | Who they're for |
|---|---|---|
| **Eventbrite** | Ticket sales + event discovery | Public events, concerts, paid attendance |
| **Partiful** | Guest invites + RSVPs (beautifully designed) | Social events, parties, Gen Z hosts |
| **Luma** | Event pages + community building | Startup events, creator communities |
| **KOOL** | Full event planning + execution | Anyone who actually *plans* events, not just invites |

**The positioning line:**
> *"Eventbrite sells tickets. Partiful handles RSVPs. KOOL plans the entire event — from first vendor call to final guest thank-you."*

KOOL is not in the invitation space. KOOL is in the **execution** space. That's a different job to be done. Lean into it hard.

### The One-Liner That Makes Someone Download Immediately

After reviewing all the copy, the competitive landscape, and the brand voice, this is the one-liner:

> **"stop planning your event across 6 apps. kool does it all."**

Why it works:
- Speaks directly to the #1 pain point (the Frankenstack problem — 4–7 disconnected tools)
- No explanation needed — the person with 6 open tabs *feels* this immediately
- Short enough for a social bio, an ad, a billboard
- Brand voice: lowercase, direct, no fluff

**Alternative for emotional resonance:**

Use the first one for acquisition. Use the second for brand storytelling.

## MANU 🔍 — Business Intelligence

### Should KOOL Launch Now or Wait for War Room + Vendor Command Center?

**Verdict: Conditional wait. Launch when 3 of these 5 milestones are met.**

Here's the framework for making this decision:

**The Early Launch Risks:**
- First impressions on the App Store are permanent. A 3.1-star rating from a buggy v1 will haunt KOOL for years.
- Event planners are professionals. If KOOL can't handle the real workflow of planning an event, they'll churn immediately and tell everyone.
- Day-of coordination is where planners live — the research is unambiguous: *"I abandon every app I own on event day."* If KOOL doesn't solve this, professionals won't convert. And professionals write the reviews that matter.
- **The Event War Room is KOOL's moat.** No competitor has it. Launching without it leaves KOOL's biggest differentiator on the table at the exact moment you're trying to acquire users.

**The Late Launch Risks:**
- Partiful and Luma are not standing still. If they ship day-of coordination features before KOOL launches, the window closes.
- Social media momentum is finite. If you build 2,000 followers pre-launch and then wait 6 more months, you'll need to rebuild it.
- Paula's personal brand in the event space is the launch vehicle. That credibility has a shelf life. Use it while the energy is live.

---

### The 5 Launch Milestones (need 3 of 5)

| # | Milestone | Status | Est. Timeline |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Core features stable (checklists, budget, vendors, RSVP, run of show) | ✅ In listing — assumed built | Now |
| 2 | ≥2,000 Instagram followers, ≥500 TikTok followers | Build in progress | 60 days |
| 3 | Event War Room in beta (even limited, even invite-only) | Not built | 90–120 days |
| 4 | 50+ TestFlight beta testers, ≥4.5 internal rating | Need to run beta | 30 days post-TestFlight |
| 5 | 3 verified reviews/testimonials from real event planners | Acquirable now via network | 30 days |

**Recommended trigger:** Launch when milestones 1, 2, 4, and 5 are complete. War Room can be "coming soon" as a premium feature — but it must be announced at launch with a timeline. Don't launch without it being on the visible roadmap.

### Pricing Strategy: Is $15/mo Right?

**Short answer: Yes, but the positioning needs work.**

**The competitive landscape:**
- Planning Pod: $49–$149/mo (too expensive, users say they use 20% of it)
- HoneyBook: $16/mo (closest competitor — also event/client management)
- Dubsado: $20/mo
- Notion (DIY): Free or $10/mo
- Partiful: Free
- Luma: Free (premium coming)

At $15/mo, KOOL is priced *exactly right* for the prosumer market — below the professional tools that cost $49–$149, above the free tools that don't actually plan events. This is intentional and correct.

**But the annual pricing needs adjustment:**
- $99/year = $8.25/mo. That's a 45% discount off monthly. That's too steep a discount.
- Recommendation: $119/year = $9.92/mo. Saves the user $60/year (vs. $81). Still compelling. Better margin.

**What to add to improve perceived value at $15/mo:**
- Name the premium plan. "KOOL Pro" or "KOOL Premium" — not just "Premium." Names create identity.
- Show the math in onboarding: *"KOOL Pro replaces tools that cost $80–$200/month combined."*
- Add one Pro-exclusive feature that free users *see but can't touch* — the "lock" visibility creates upgrade intent without blocking core value.

**One pricing concept to consider for the future:**
The competitive report identified that planners hate per-seat pricing. Consider a **per-event** pricing tier for casual users (e.g., $9.99/event, unlimited guests and features for that one event). This captures the market of people who only plan 2–3 events per year and won't pay monthly — and converts them when they become frequent planners.

### The Single Milestone That Should Trigger App Store Submission

**When the TestFlight beta exits with a ≥4.5/5 average rating from 25+ real users, submit to the App Store.**

Not before. Not based on a calendar date. Not because social media says you're ready. The product has to be ready.

Everything else — social buildup, press, influencer relationships, Product Hunt prep — runs in parallel. But the submission button only gets pressed when real planners have used KOOL to plan real events and said *"yes, this is what I needed."*

That's the only signal that matters.

— *Manu 🔍, Research Director, TKG*
