## 01 | what manners actually mean

[IMAGE: Close-up of a woman's gloved hand resting lightly on a man's forearm during a formal event. Black and white. Soft focus on background chandeliers.]

manners are not a performance. they are not a costume you put on when the cameras are rolling and take off when the car arrives.

manners are the daily practice of treating other human beings as worthy of your full attention, your care, and your respect.

emily post famously said that etiquette is not about rules — it's about *consideration*. i believe that. but i'd go one step further: manners are a form of love. they are how we say, without words, *you matter. i see you. you are worth my best.*

at a high-stakes event, this translation becomes critical. a guest who is warmly greeted at the door feels entirely different about the evening than one who was left searching for their seat. a speaker who is properly introduced carries themselves with confidence. a table that is thoughtfully set communicates, before a single word is spoken, that the host is someone who cares.

> *"manners are the visible reflection of your invisible character. when you walk into a room knowing how to conduct yourself, you give yourself permission to be fully present — and that is when the real magic happens."*
> **— paula mescolin**

manners are not elite. they are not reserved for the wealthy, the titled, or the formally educated. they are available to every person who chooses to be intentional about how they show up.

the koolture group believes that **excellence is accessible** — and that this guide is your entry point.

## 02 | the foundational do's and don'ts

[IMAGE: An elegant event entrance — velvet rope, soft lighting, well-dressed attendees arriving. Black and white. One attendee caught mid-laugh with another. Candid but composed.]

### do's — the non-negotiables

- **do arrive prepared.** know the event, the host, and at least three people you plan to speak with before you arrive.
- **do silence your phone completely** when greeting hosts, at the dinner table, and during speeches or ceremonies.
- **do introduce yourself clearly** — your name, spoken at a moderate pace, with eye contact.
- **do write things down** (or note them in your phone *away from the table*) — names, commitments, follow-ups.
- **do eat before a networking event** if you're not sure food will be provided.
- **do accept hospitality graciously** — "no thank you" is sufficient. a lengthy explanation of your diet is not required.
- **do send a thank you** within 24–48 hours. always.
- **do dress to the specified code** — it is a form of respect to the host.
- **do stand when being introduced** to someone older, more senior, or of higher rank.
- **do use names** — people love to hear their own name. use it twice in a new conversation.

### don'ts — the ones that will cost you

- **don't arrive early to a hosted dinner** — it puts the host in an impossible position. five minutes after the stated time is ideal.
- **don't monopolize the host** — greet them, express your gratitude, and let them circulate.
- **don't talk over anyone**, interrupt, or finish someone's sentence.
- **don't drink excessively** at professional or formal events. ever.
- **don't discuss money, religion, or politics** unless you know your audience extremely well — and even then, read the room.
- **don't photograph food or the table** without the host's acknowledgment.
- **don't bring uninvited guests** without prior approval.
- **don't RSVP yes and not show up.** this is one of the great etiquette crimes of our time.
- **don't ignore the support staff** — servers, valets, coat check attendants deserve the same courtesy as the CEO.
- **don't look around the room while someone is talking to you.** it is a dismissal dressed up as nothing.

## 03 | how to greet guests

[IMAGE: A host — elegant, poised — extending both hands to greet a guest at the entrance of a warmly lit event space. Black and white. Genuine warmth visible in the composition.]

the greeting sets the entire emotional temperature of the event. it is the first impression, the first feeling, the first promise you make to your guest.

### the host's responsibility

as a host, you (or a designated greeter) should be **at the entrance** for the first 20–30 minutes of any formal event. guests should never walk in to an empty doorway, unsure if they're in the right place.

your greeting sequence:
1. **eye contact** before they reach you
2. **smile** — real, warm, not performative
3. **step forward** to meet them (never wait to be approached)
4. **call them by name** if you know them
5. **handshake or embrace** (read the relationship)
6. **introduce them** to someone nearby immediately — never leave a new arrival standing alone

### the greeting itself

**formal settings:** a firm handshake, right hand, direct eye contact, "good evening, [name]. i'm so glad you're here."

**semi-formal / social settings:** a handshake or two-cheek air kiss depending on culture and relationship. in latin, european, and many international circles, a two-cheek greeting is standard. in american corporate settings, the handshake remains the professional norm.

**never:** a limp handshake. a half-hug to someone you've just met. a greeting from across the room with a wave. these all communicate *you're not a priority.*

### greeting dignitaries, clergy, or public figures

see section 04 for specific forms of address. the greeting protocol is the same, with one addition: **wait to be addressed first** if you are not the host or an official greeter. introduce yourself only if the dignitary's aide or host initiates it.

> *"i've watched the entire energy of an event shift because the host stood at the door and looked every single guest in the eye. you cannot overestimate what it means to truly be seen when you walk in."*
> **— paula mescolin**

## 04 | addressing dignitaries

[IMAGE: A formal reception line — men and women in formal attire, a decorated military officer exchanging greetings. Editorial composition. Black and white.]

getting the address right is not about being stuffy. it's about honoring the work and service of the individual in front of you.

### government & elected officials

| title | spoken address | written address |
|---|---|---|
| the president | mr./madam president | the president of the united states |
| vice president | mr./madam vice president | the honorable [full name] |
| senator | senator [last name] | the honorable [full name], united states senator |
| governor | governor [last name] | the honorable [full name], governor of [state] |
| mayor | mayor [last name] | the honorable [full name], mayor of [city] |
| judge | your honor / judge [last name] | the honorable [full name] |

### military officers

address by rank and last name: "general powell," "admiral jones," "colonel harris." when in doubt, "sir" or "ma'am" is never wrong.

### religious figures

| figure | spoken address |
|---|---|
| pope | your holiness |
| cardinal | your eminence |
| bishop | your excellency or bishop [last name] |
| rabbi | rabbi [last name] |
| reverend / pastor | reverend [last name] or pastor [last name] |

### royalty & nobility

- king/queen: "your majesty" (first reference), then "sir" or "ma'am"
- prince/princess: "your royal highness"
- duke/duchess: "your grace"
- lord/lady: "my lord" or "my lady" (or simply their title + surname at introduction)

**the golden rule:** when in doubt, default to the most formal address. you will never offend someone by showing more respect than necessary. you can offend by showing less.

## 05 | invitations: addressing families, couples & individuals

[IMAGE: A hand-addressed cream envelope, wax seal, fountain pen resting nearby. Black and white close-up. Luxurious texture visible in the paper.]

the invitation is the first tangible piece of your event that a guest receives. it signals everything about the occasion before they've set foot in the room.

### the envelope hierarchy

the outer envelope is formal. the inner envelope (if used) is where you list exactly who is invited.

**a married couple, same last name:**
Mr. and Mrs. Paulo Mescolin
*(traditionalist form — still the most formal and widely accepted)*

**a married couple, different last names:**
Ms. Paula Marques and Mr. Paulo Mescolin
*(list the person with whom you have the primary relationship first)*

**same-sex couples:**
follow the same rule — list alphabetically or by the strength of your relationship with each. either name may come first.

**a couple who are not married:**
Ms. Paula Marques
Mr. Paulo Mescolin
*(list on separate lines of the same envelope, with your closer contact first)*

**a family with children (inner envelope only):**
Mr. and Mrs. Mescolin
Maria, Pedro
*(children listed by age, oldest first — and only listed if they are invited)*

**a single person you'd like to bring a guest:**
Ms. Paula Marques and Guest

**a single person, no guest intended:**
Ms. Paula Marques
*(the absence of "and guest" is intentional and clear)*

**professional titles:**
always use them. Dr. Sarah Leigh. Judge Marcus Williams. Captain Ana Ferreira.

### the anatomy of a formal invitation

1. **the host line** — "The Koolture Group requests the honor of your presence..."
2. **the occasion line** — what is being celebrated or commemorated
3. **the date and time** — spelled out formally ("Saturday, the twenty-first of June")
4. **the location** — full venue name and address
5. **the dress code** — lower right corner of the invitation
6. **rsvp information** — separate enclosure card or lower left corner

> *"an invitation is a promise. it says: i thought of you specifically. you were chosen for this room. make it feel that way."*
> **— paula mescolin**

## 06 | seating rules that actually work

[IMAGE: An empty banquet room — round tables perfectly laid, escort cards in a row on a table near the entrance. Black and white. Anticipation in the stillness.]

### the principles

seating is strategy. it is one of the most underestimated tools a host has to shape the energy, conversation, and outcome of an event.

**rule one: never seat people randomly at a formal dinner.** every seat assignment is a curatorial decision.

**rule two: separate couples at dinner parties** (of 8 or more). you both came with someone you already know. seating is your opportunity to create new connections.

**rule three: honor the guest of honor** — they sit at the host's right. the second guest of honor sits at the host's left.

**rule four: seat enemies apart and strategically.** if you know two guests have tension, you are responsible for managing the proximity.

**rule five: consider mobility, hearing, and sight lines** when assigning seats for elderly guests or those with accessibility needs.

### formal place cards

use place cards at any seated dinner of six or more. they signal intentionality and prevent the uncomfortable hovering guests experience when unsure where to sit.

**escort cards** (at the entrance) → tell guests their table.
**place cards** (on the table) → tell guests their specific seat.

at a formal event, both are required. at semi-formal dinners, place cards alone are sufficient.

### the head table

for galas, weddings, and official banquets, the head table seats the hosts, guest(s) of honor, and any dignitaries. it typically faces the room. speakers, if different from the hosts, may be seated at the head table or at a visible position nearby.

## 07 | the run of show

[IMAGE: A production desk backstage — run of show printout, headset, stopwatch, dim work light. Black and white. The controlled chaos behind the curtain.]

the run of show (ROS) is the backbone of every professional event. it is not a suggestion. it is a contract with your guests' time.

### structure of a professional ROS

every run of show document should include:

- **event name, date, venue, production contact**
- **time-stamped sequence** — every element, every transition, every speaker
- **responsible party** for each line item
- **buffer time** built in (typically 10–15 minutes across a 3-hour event)
- **contingency notes** — what happens if [X] runs over or [Y] doesn't arrive

### timing etiquette

- **doors open** → guests should be received, music playing, staff in position
- **program start** → no more than 15 minutes after stated time (10 is better)
- **speeches and presentations** → every speaker gets a hard stop time and should be told in advance
- **meals** → never serve a course during a speech. transitions between food and program must be coordinated
- **program end** → always end on time or early. never run over. your guests have lives.

### etiquette rules for everyone in the ROS

for **speakers:** arrive early. meet your AV contact. know your time limit. respect it.

for **performers:** be in place 30 minutes before your call. what happens offstage reflects on the event.

for **hosts and emcees:** your energy sets the tone. never apologize on stage for things going wrong. pivot with grace.

> *"the best run of show is the one no one notices — because everything flowed so beautifully, the guests thought it was effortless. that's the goal."*
> **— paula mescolin**

## 08 | how to set a table

[IMAGE: An overhead flat-lay of a single formal place setting — bone china, crystal, silver. Everything aligned perfectly. Black and white. Near-architectural symmetry.]

### the informal place setting (dinner parties, casual corporate)

left of the plate: **fork**
right of the plate: **knife** (blade facing plate) and **spoon**
above the plate: **dessert fork and spoon** (horizontal)
top right: **water glass**, then **wine glass(es)** to the right and slightly lower
plate center: **charger or dinner plate**
upper left: **bread plate** with **butter knife** resting diagonally

### the formal place setting (galas, banquets, state dinners)

work from outside in — the outermost utensils are used first.

**left of plate (outside in):** salad fork, dinner fork, fish fork
**right of plate (outside in):** fish knife, dinner knife, salad knife, soup spoon
**above plate:** dessert fork (tines right) and dessert spoon (bowl left)
**glassware (outside in toward plate):** water goblet, white wine, red wine, champagne flute

### the non-negotiables

- napkin goes to the left of the forks, or on the charger — never tucked under the plate
- no utensil should be more than 1 inch from the edge of the table
- glasses are placed at the tip of the dinner knife
- no item should touch another — spacing is part of the presentation

## 09 | the art of using utensils

[IMAGE: A man's hands — elegant, unhurried — using a knife and fork properly over a white plate. Black and white. Quiet authority.]

### the two schools

**american style:** cut food, place knife on plate, switch fork to right hand to eat.
**continental (european) style:** fork stays in left hand (tines down), knife in right. eat as you cut. this is considered the more refined approach internationally.

at formal international events, **continental style is the standard.**

### the signals your utensils send

- **"i'm resting"** → fork and knife placed in an inverted V on the plate (4 and 8 o'clock position)
- **"i'm finished"** → fork and knife placed parallel, handles at 4 o'clock, diagonally across the plate
- **the napkin** → placed on your chair when leaving temporarily. placed to the left of your plate when the meal is over. never on the plate.

### specific utensils

**soup:** spoon moves away from you, sip from the side — never slurp. tip the bowl away from you to get the last spoonfuls.

**fish:** use the fish knife and fish fork, both provided in formal settings.

**shellfish:** oyster fork is placed to the right of the soup spoon, outside all other utensils.

**bread:** always break with your hands, never cut. butter one piece at a time.

## 10 | etiquette for ladies

[IMAGE: A woman in an elegant off-shoulder gown entering a formal event. Photographed from behind. Black and white. Strength and grace in equal measure.]

grace is not weakness. it is the most powerful form of presence.

- **your posture** is your first statement. enter every room with shoulders back, chin level. not stiff — poised.
- **your entrance** should be unhurried. rushing communicates panic. a measured pace communicates authority.
- when seated at a table, sit close to the edge — back straight, not resting against the chair at formal events.
- **your handbag** belongs on your lap, on the back of your chair, or on a bag hook (always request one at formal dinners) — never on the table or the floor.
- when a gentleman rises to seat you, allow it. it is not a diminishment. it is an honor that you receive with grace.
- **your phone** is invisible at a formal table. no exceptions.
- in conversation: listen fully. do not interrupt. do not over-explain.
- **your scent** should arrive with you, not before you. heavy fragrance at events is inconsiderate.
- accept compliments with a simple, warm "thank you." deflecting compliments is a form of rejection.
- if your heel catches, your dress tears, or your glass spills — handle it calmly, make light of it briefly, and move on. the room will follow your energy.

> *"a woman with etiquette doesn't need to demand respect. she's already communicated that she expects it — and why she deserves it."*
> **— paula mescolin**

## 11 | etiquette for gentlemen

[IMAGE: A man in a well-cut suit adjusting his cufflinks before entering an event. Black and white. The ritual of preparation.]

a gentleman is not defined by the cut of his suit. he is defined by the quality of his attention.

- **dress with intention.** a gentleman's attire communicates respect for the event and the host. when in doubt, err formal.
- **arrive on time.** a gentleman does not make people wait.
- **rise when introduced** to women, elders, dignitaries, or anyone deserving of recognition.
- **open doors** — for everyone. this is not dated. this is decency.
- **pull out the chair** for your companion at a formal dinner.
- **make the introduction** when you are the common denominator between two people — never let a silence stretch while people wait to be introduced.
- at the table, **wait until all guests are seated and served** before beginning to eat.
- **do not speak over, across, or around** the person next to you. give your attention wholly.
- your handshake is your first impression. **firm, brief, direct eye contact.**
- when a woman excuses herself from the table, **rise slightly** in acknowledgment. when she returns, do the same.
- **your phone is in your pocket.** it stays there.

## 12 | the right verbiage

[IMAGE: A close-up of handwritten event program script on cream paper. Fountain pen beside it. Black and white.]

words are architecture. choose them carefully.

### phrases that elevate

- *"it's a pleasure to meet you"* — more intentional than "nice to meet you"
- *"please allow me to introduce..."* — formal, deliberate, respectful
- *"i'd be honored"* — accepts an invitation with warmth and intention
- *"how may i assist you?"* — better than "can i help you?"
- *"thank you for the thoughtfulness"* — acknowledges effort, not just outcome
- *"forgive me, i didn't catch your name"* — gracious recovery
- *"what an extraordinary evening"* — specific gratitude to a host

### phrases to retire

- "no problem" → replace with "of course" or "my pleasure"
- "you guys" → "you all," "everyone," or by name
- "honestly" as a filler → it implies you aren't always honest
- "to be frank" → same issue
- "i know, right?" → dismisses the other person's thought rather than engaging it
- "i'll try" → either you will or you won't. commit or decline.

### event-specific verbiage

**invitations:** use "requests the honor of your presence" for ceremonies, "requests the pleasure of your company" for social events. these distinctions matter.

**toasts:** open by welcoming the room, state your relationship to the honoree, share one specific story or quality, close with a wish. never apologize for what you're about to say.

**introductions on stage:** name, title, organization, one specific achievement. end with applause cue. practice it.

## 13 | chivalry — why it still matters

[IMAGE: A man holding open a heavy door, a woman walking through, both smiling. Black and white. Neither deferential nor transactional — simply human.]

chivalry was never about hierarchy. it was about protection and honor in practice.

in its modern form, chivalry is simply this: **noticing what someone needs and providing it before they have to ask.**

it is a gentleman who stands when a woman enters the room — not because she can't stand on her own, but because he recognizes that she is worth the gesture.

it is a woman who writes a handwritten note — not because email doesn't work, but because she knows that pen on paper says *i took time for you.*

it is anyone who helps the older guest to their seat, refills the water without being asked, holds the elevator, or notices that someone is standing alone at a party and goes to them.

chivalry is not antiquated. chivalry is one of the most revolutionary acts in a world that has forgotten that **the smallest courtesies carry the most weight.**

> *"people remember how you made them feel. not your centerpieces. not your catering. how you made them feel. chivalry is the most precise tool you have for getting that right."*
> **— paula mescolin**

## 14 | the importance of being on time

[IMAGE: A classic analog clock on a marble mantle. Candlelight. Event in soft focus behind it. Black and white.]

punctuality is respect made visible.

when you are late, you are communicating — whether you mean to or not — that your time is more valuable than the time of everyone waiting for you.

### for guests

- **formal events:** arrive within 5–10 minutes of the stated time. no earlier, no later.
- **dinner parties:** 5–10 minutes after the stated arrival time is correct. arriving exactly on time puts pressure on the host.
- **if you will be late:** call or message the host *in advance*, not from the parking lot. give a specific time. keep it.
- **if you cannot attend:** contact the host as early as possible. the later you cancel, the more costly it is to them — logistically and emotionally.

### for hosts & planners

- **program start time** should be honored within 10 minutes. any longer and you've broken faith with your audience.
- **build buffer into your timeline.** the run of show should include it. do not communicate the buffer to speakers — it is yours, not theirs.
- **if you're running behind:** acknowledge it once, briefly, on stage. do not apologize repeatedly. get moving.

### for speakers & presenters

- arrive at least 30 minutes before your speaking slot.
- speak to the AV team before the program starts.
- **end on time.** every minute you run over is a minute stolen from every person in that room.

## 15 | body language at events

[IMAGE: Two people in deep conversation at a cocktail reception — leaning in slightly, eye contact, drinks held low. Black and white. Full presence captured in the composition.]

your body speaks before your mouth opens. at events, everyone is watching — and most of them don't know it.

**the open stance:** feet shoulder-width apart, arms uncrossed, weight evenly distributed. this is the stance of someone comfortable in their environment and open to connection.

**eye contact:** hold it for 3–4 seconds at a time in conversation. darting eyes communicate disinterest or anxiety. too long communicates aggression. the 3-4 second rule is connection.

**the handshake zone:** extend your hand into the space between you and the other person — not reaching toward them, not waiting for them to close the gap. meet in the middle.

**mirroring:** unconsciously, we mirror the body language of people we like and trust. consciously, you can use subtle mirroring to build rapport — matching posture, pace, and energy level.

**don't:** cross your arms (defensive), check your phone while in conversation (dismissive), stand with your back to the room (closes you off from connection), or hover at the edge of a space (communicates uncertainty).

**do:** angle your body toward the person speaking. nod to show engagement. use the person's name naturally. smile with your eyes.

## 16 | networking with intention

[IMAGE: A networking event — two professionals exchanging business cards with both hands (Japanese-style, a gesture of respect). Black and white.]

the word "networking" has been cheapened. it implies transaction. what we're really talking about is **cultivating meaningful relationships in concentrated time.**

### before you arrive

- identify three people you want to meet or reconnect with
- have your introduction ready: your name, your role, and one genuine question about the other person
- bring business cards or have a digital card ready (QR-based cards are increasingly standard at premium events)

### the approach

never interrupt a closed conversation (two people facing each other directly). approach open groups (two people at an angle) by making eye contact and asking to join.

lead with curiosity, not pitch. "what brings you to this event?" is better than "let me tell you what i do."

### the 2-minute rule

most first conversations at events should run 2–3 minutes before naturally transitioning or exchanging contact information. longer isn't always better. a meaningful 2-minute exchange leads to a follow-up. a rambling 15-minute conversation can close doors.

### the graceful exit

"i've been looking forward to connecting with [name] — would you excuse me? i'd love to continue this conversation — may i have your card?"

never simply back away or say "i'm going to get a drink" and disappear.

### the follow-up

the connection was made at the event. the relationship is built the next day.
follow up within 24 hours. be specific. reference something from your conversation. no generic "great to meet you."

## 17 | gifting with grace

[IMAGE: A beautifully wrapped gift — black box, white ribbon, small card handwritten. Black and white. Deliberate, considered, beautiful.]

a gift is a symbol. it says: i thought of you when you weren't in front of me.

### host gifts

always bring a host gift to a private dinner. wine is appropriate only if you know the host's preferences. consider: artisan candles, curated chocolates, a book with a personal inscription, flowers in a vase (not requiring immediate arranging).

**do not bring:** a gift that requires immediate opening, preparation, or integration into the event.

### corporate & event gifts

- align with the brand and purpose of the event
- personalization dramatically increases perceived value
- avoid anything with your own logo at another person's event
- think *useful + memorable* — not expensive for the sake of it

### when you receive a gift

receive it with your full attention. if it's wrapped, ask whether the giver would like you to open it now. say something specific about what the gift represents to you — not just "thank you, i love it" but "this is so thoughtful — i know you remembered that i mentioned [x]."

### gifting across cultures

be aware that in some cultures (many asian and middle eastern traditions), gifts are not opened immediately in front of the giver. in others, refusing a gift is deeply offensive. when in doubt, research or ask a cultural liaison in advance.

> *"the best gift you can give anyone at your event is the feeling that they were seen. everything else is beautiful decoration."*
> **— paula mescolin**

## 18 | the thank you note

[IMAGE: A hand writing a note on thick cream stationery. A cup of espresso in the soft background. Black and white. Intimacy and intention.]

this section could be the most important in the entire guide.

the thank you note is nearly extinct. which means that anyone who writes one is immediately extraordinary.

### the rules

**timing:** within 24–48 hours of the event. within the week for gifts received.

**medium:** handwritten, on quality stationery, is the gold standard. a thoughtful email is the acceptable alternative. a text is a last resort — and only for close relationships.

**structure:**
1. name the specific event or gift
2. say what it meant to you — not generically, but personally
3. reference something specific from the evening
4. express your hope for continued connection

**example:**
*"dear paulo — last evening at the gala was genuinely one of the most beautifully curated events i've attended this year. the way you opened the program with the children's choir set a tone that carried throughout the night. i left feeling inspired and honored to be included. please know that your thoughtfulness did not go unnoticed. warmly, paula."*

**what never to write:** a form letter. a copy-paste message. something that could have been written to anyone in the room.

## 19 | dress codes decoded ✦

[IMAGE: Three outfits side by side — black tie, cocktail, and smart casual, all photographed on tailored mannequins. Black and white. Editorial fashion editorial quality.]

one of the most common sources of event anxiety — and one of the least addressed in etiquette guides — is the dress code.

### the hierarchy

**white tie** (ultra-formal): men wear tailcoats, white waistcoat, white bow tie. women wear full-length ball gowns. this is the most formal dress code in existence and is rare outside state occasions and galas.

**black tie**: men wear a tuxedo with black bow tie. women wear a floor-length or elegant cocktail gown. this is the standard formal event code.

**black tie optional / creative black tie**: men may wear a dark suit. women have more flexibility — elegant midi lengths are acceptable. "creative" means fashion-forward interpretations of the formal palette.

**cocktail attire**: men wear a suit and tie (or smart blazer). women wear knee-to-midi length dresses or elegant separates. this is the most common upscale event code.

**smart casual**: elevated everyday dress. no jeans, no sneakers, no athletic wear. collared shirts for men. polished separates or casual dresses for women.

**resort casual / garden party**: light fabrics, warmer palettes, but still intentional and polished.

### the host's responsibility

**always state the dress code on the invitation.** the absence of a dress code causes more anxiety than any other element. if your event has a theme or color preference, communicate that too.

if a guest calls to ask what to wear: answer directly. never say "whatever you're comfortable in" — it's not helpful.

## 20 | digital etiquette: phones, social media & photography ✦

[IMAGE: A dinner table scene — glasses of wine, candlelight, beautiful place settings. In the center of the frame, a small tray or basket where guests have placed their phones face-down. Black and white. The intentional absence is the statement.]

we have not collectively agreed on the rules for devices at events. that gap has produced some of the worst etiquette violations of our time. let's address it clearly.

### for guests: the phone rule

at a formal or business dinner: **your phone is off the table.** physically off the table. in your pocket, your bag, or the provided phone pouch (see below).

the "phone on the table face-down" is still a phone on the table. it still says *something might interrupt this.* it still says *you're not fully here.*

**at a cocktail hour:** phone use for calls or professional follow-ups is acceptable, handled briefly and privately.

**if you are expecting a critical call:** notify the host in advance. step away when the call comes. do not answer at the table.

### for hosts: setting the digital tone

consider implementing one of these approaches:
- **phone-free cards** at each place setting (a simple, elegant card stating the policy)
- **a basket or pouch at the entrance** for phones during dinner (make it a ritual, not a rule)
- **a social media moment** — designate a specific time for guests to photograph, then signal its end

### photography

**professional photography:** always brief your photographer on restricted moments (private toasts, emotional conversations, VIP moments). guests should not photograph while the professional photographer is working a scene.

**guest photography:** acceptable in social settings. never pointed at someone without their awareness. never at a eulogy, prayer, or private ceremony without explicit permission.

**posting on social media:** never post photos of other guests without their awareness. never post ahead of the host's official release if it's a professional event.

## 21 | toasts & speeches ✦

[IMAGE: A person rising from a banquet table, champagne glass raised, guests watching attentively. Black and white. The held breath before words.]

### the structure of a great toast

1. **rise, raise your glass, and command attention** — don't begin speaking until the room is quiet
2. **introduce yourself** (briefly) if not everyone knows you
3. **state your relationship** to the honoree or event
4. **one story** — specific, true, revealing of character
5. **the wish** — your heartfelt hope for the person or occasion
6. **the lift** — "please join me in raising your glass to..."
7. **drink with the room**

### the rules

- a toast should be **90 seconds to 3 minutes.** maximum.
- **never toast yourself** if you are the guest of honor. receive toasts graciously, eyes on the room.
- **wait until all glasses are filled** before beginning.
- **if you don't drink alcohol**, raise your water glass. it's entirely correct.
- **the best man/maid of honor** toast at a wedding is the template everyone else follows. it should be the longest at 3–4 minutes. all others: shorter.
- **never read directly from your phone.** notes on paper are acceptable. memorized is best.

## 22 | dietary needs & special accommodations ✦

[IMAGE: A caterer adjusting a custom plated dish with a small card beside it indicating dietary note. Black and white. Behind-the-scenes care made visible.]

the way a host handles the invisible needs of their guests is the truest measure of their excellence.

### for hosts

- include a **dietary section on your RSVP** — always. options: vegetarian, vegan, gluten-free, kosher, halal, nut allergy, other.
- **communicate confirmed dietary needs to your caterer** at least one week in advance.
- at a plated dinner, **mark dietary-specific plates** so service is seamless (the guest never has to wave a hand or announce a restriction at the table).
- ensure there are **visible options** at buffets and cocktail receptions for guests with restrictions — and that they are genuinely filling, not just a side salad.
- **accessibility:** consider mobility for seating assignments. ensure accessible entry points are attended by staff, not just signage.

### for guests

- communicate dietary needs **on your RSVP** — not at the event.
- if you have an allergy (not a preference), also confirm directly with the host 48 hours in advance.
- do not make your dietary needs the subject of conversation at the table. simply manage your plate gracefully and move on.
- **never assume.** always confirm.

## 23 | the graceful exit ✦

[IMAGE: A couple saying goodbye at the door — the host, warm and gracious, hands extended. Guests departing into the evening. Black and white. The afterglow of a beautiful event.]

how you leave is part of how you're remembered.

### the guest's responsibility

- **don't disappear.** always say goodbye to the host. always.
- **don't leave during a speech, toast, or ceremony** unless there is an emergency.
- the appropriate time to leave a formal dinner: after dessert has been served and the first guests begin to depart.
- at a cocktail or networking event: after 90 minutes is appropriate. two hours is generous.
- **your exit conversation with the host** should be warm, specific, and brief: "this was an exceptional evening. the [specific detail] was extraordinary. thank you for including me."

### for hosts

- **a strong close is as important as a strong open.** plan your event's ending.
- consider: a closing toast, a gift at departure, a specific farewell moment from the stage.
- have staff **stationed near the exit** to assist with coats, cars, and a final goodbye.
- **never make guests feel rushed** — but give clear signals when the program has concluded.
- the host exits last. always.

## 24 | navigating multicultural events ✦

[IMAGE: A diverse group of formally dressed guests engaged in warm conversation — different ethnicities, ages, styles. Black and white. The beautiful complexity of a well-curated room.]

the world is your guest list. treat it that way.

### before the event

research the primary cultures represented among your guests. a 30-minute reading can prevent a significant misstep.

key areas to understand:
- **greeting customs** (handshake? bow? two-cheek kiss? no physical contact?)
- **dietary restrictions** rooted in religious observance (kosher, halal, hindu, jain, fasting practices)
- **gender dynamics** in professional and social settings
- **gift-giving customs** if gifts are part of the event
- **punctuality expectations** (some cultures have fluid timing norms — plan accordingly)
- **titles and forms of address** — some cultures place family name first

### in the moment

if you are unsure of a custom: observe, follow the lead of the other person, and ask graciously when appropriate ("is there a particular way you prefer to be greeted?").

**never:** express surprise or amusement at a cultural difference. never make someone feel like a representative of their entire culture.

**always:** create an environment where every guest — regardless of background — feels that the event was designed with them in mind.

## 25 | the host's invisible rule book ✦

[IMAGE: A host, glass in hand, surveying the room from a slight distance — watching, orchestrating, present. Black and white. The invisible architecture of the event is her.]

the things that make an event extraordinary are almost never visible. they are felt.

this is the section no other etiquette guide will give you, because these things cannot be researched. they can only be lived.

**the 10 things extraordinary hosts do that guests never notice:**

1. **they eat before the event.** the host who is hungry cannot be fully present.

2. **they designate a second.** someone besides the host who knows the full run of show and can make decisions without disruption.

3. **they brief their service team on VIPs.** servers know which guests prefer water refilled silently, which guests require accessible navigation, which table seats the dignitary.

4. **they have an emergency kit.** stain remover, safety pins, extra name cards, double-sided tape, pain reliever, mints, phone charger. it lives with the coordinator, invisible and indispensable.

5. **they check in on solo guests.** anyone who arrived alone gets a check-in from the host or an appointed connector within the first 20 minutes.

6. **they don't hover over unhappy situations.** when something goes wrong, the extraordinary host addresses it quietly, efficiently, and moves on — without announcing the recovery.

7. **they close every event with intention.** a closing toast. a moment of reflection. a specific thank-you. the end of the event is part of the experience.

8. **they protect their vendors.** guests who are rude to staff are quietly redirected. the team that makes the event work deserves dignity.

9. **they are the calm.** when things go sideways — and they always do — the host's expression and energy communicate to every guest whether to panic or to trust. be the calm.

10. **they follow up.** within 48 hours, the host reaches out to the guest of honor, the key speakers, and the guests who traveled farthest. this is the final act of hospitality, and it is almost never done.
